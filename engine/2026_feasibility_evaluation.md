## Executive Summary

This document evaluates the feasibility of the American Liberation Movement achieving its desired outcomes in the 2026 midterm elections: (1) electing independent candidates to Congress, and (2) persuading incumbent legislators to switch their affiliation to independent. 

Our analysis concludes that while the political environment is ripe with unprecedented levels of dissatisfaction with the two-party system, the structural barriers to independent success in the United States are immense. Direct electoral victory in 2026 is a highly improbable primary goal. Specifically:

*   **Electing new independents** is possible only in rare, "perfect storm" scenarios and should be viewed as an exceptional, not a primary, objective.
*   **Persuading incumbents to switch** is a strategically flawed objective with no historical precedent for success in the modern era; incumbents who abandon their party's infrastructure invariably lose their power base and their seat.

A more viable strategy, grounded in the work of Gene Sharp, would reframe the 2026 midterms not as an endpoint for winning seats, but as a **fulcrum for demonstrating power**. The movement's focus should shift from winning within the current system to disrupting it, delegitimizing it, and building the foundation of an alternative. This involves strategically targeting a small number of races to create disproportionate impact, converting the vast, existing anti-Trump protest energy into a focused anti-two-party movement, and beginning the long-term work of building parallel institutions that can eventually supplant the old ones. The opportunity is not to win the 2026 election, but to use it as a powerful tool in a much longer nonviolent struggle.

## 1. Introduction: The 2026 Midterm Challenge

The American Liberation Movement, as articulated on liberate.us, identifies the two-party political system as the fundamental source of political dysfunction and public disenfranchisement in the United States. The proposed strategy is a national political strike, a mass withdrawal of consent, to force the dissolution of this duopoly. This evaluation assesses the movement's potential to translate this strategy into tangible success within the 2026 midterm election cycle. 

This analysis is structured into four parts:
1.  **The Opportunity:** An examination of the favorable conditions, including record-high anti-party sentiment and a massive, mobilized protest movement.
2.  **The Obstacles:** A clear-eyed look at the powerful structural, financial, and psychological barriers that protect the two-party system.
3.  **An Evaluation of the Stated Goals:** A data-driven assessment of the two primary objectives: electing independents and flipping incumbents.
4.  **Strategic Recommendations:** A revised path forward, applying the principles of Gene Sharp to leverage the 2026 election cycle for long-term strategic gain.

## 2. The Opportunity: A Climate of Discontent

The current political landscape presents a fertile ground for a movement challenging the two-party status quo. Several key factors create a significant window of opportunity.

### Record-High Dissatisfaction

Public sentiment against the two major parties is at an all-time high. Polling data from late 2025 and early 2026 paints a stark picture:

*   **A record 45% of Americans now identify as political independents**, according to Gallup, surpassing the previous highs of 43% [1]. This is not a fleeting trend; the percentage of independents has consistently been over 40% for more than a decade.
*   A significant majority, **62% of Americans, believe a third party is needed** because the Republican and Democratic parties do "such a poor job" of representing the American people [2].
*   This dissatisfaction is not merely passive. In a spring 2025 Pew Research Center poll, **62% of Americans were dissatisfied with the way democracy is working** in the country [3].

This widespread discontent provides a massive, addressable audience for a movement that offers a credible alternative to the current system.

### A Mobilized Populace and the 3.5% Threshold

The most significant asset for any nonviolent movement is the active participation of the populace. Research by Erica Chenoweth at Harvard University has shown that **no nonviolent movement in history has failed after achieving active participation from just 3.5% of the population** [4].

Crucially, the data suggests the United States may have already crossed this threshold in terms of anti-Trump protest mobilization. Since January 2025, an estimated **10 to 15 million Americans have participated in protests against the administration** [5]. With a U.S. population of approximately 335 million, the 3.5% threshold translates to about 11.7 million people. The "No Kings Day" protests in October 2025 alone mobilized an estimated 5-6 million people in a single day.

This demonstrates two critical facts:
1.  There is a massive, existing, and recently activated grassroots infrastructure capable of organizing large-scale demonstrations.
2.  A significant portion of the population is willing to take to the streets to challenge what they perceive as a threat to democracy.

The strategic challenge, which will be addressed later, is to **channel this existing anti-Trump energy into a broader anti-two-party system movement**.

## 3. The Obstacles: The Two-Party Fortress

Despite the favorable climate of discontent, the American two-party system is a deeply entrenched fortress, protected by formidable structural, financial, and psychological barriers that are explicitly designed to prevent the success of outsiders.

### Structural Barriers

The very mechanics of American elections are designed to perpetuate a two-party duopoly.

*   **Duverger's Law:** This political science principle states that single-member districts with first-past-the-post (or winner-take-all) voting systems, like those used in the U.S., inevitably lead to a two-party system. Voters are incentivized to engage in "strategic voting," choosing the "lesser of two evils" rather than a preferred third option they fear will "waste" their vote.
*   **Ballot Access Laws:** Independent and third-party candidates face Herculean tasks to simply appear on the ballot. While major party candidates often qualify with a simple filing fee, independents must gather thousands, sometimes tens of thousands, of signatures, a costly and labor-intensive process that varies by state [6].
*   **Sore Loser Laws:** In 48 states, a candidate who loses a major party's primary is legally barred from running in the general election as an independent [7]. This law effectively prevents a popular insurgent from taking their movement directly to the voters if they are defeated by the party establishment.
*   **Congressional Power Structure:** Power in Congress is entirely controlled by the two parties. Committee assignments, which are essential for any legislative influence, are allocated by party leadership. An independent who refuses to caucus with either party is rendered legislatively impotent, as demonstrated by the case of Justin Amash, who was stripped of his committee assignments after leaving the Republican party [8].

### Financial Barriers

Modern elections are extraordinarily expensive. The 2026 midterms are projected to be the most expensive in history [9]. The two major parties operate as massive, national fundraising machines, providing their candidates with a financial infrastructure that is nearly impossible for an independent to replicate.

| Party Advantage | Independent Disadvantage |
| :--- | :--- |
| National donor databases and networks | Must build fundraising from scratch |
| Coordinated PAC and Super PAC spending | Isolated from major funding streams |
| Shared media buys and consulting resources | Higher costs for all campaign services |
| Brand recognition and voter familiarity | Must build name ID from zero |

### Psychological Barriers: The "Spoiler Effect"

The most powerful obstacle is the psychological fear of the "spoiler effect." Even among the 62% of Americans who desire a third party, there is profound hesitation to act on that desire. A recent Gallup poll revealed this critical gap:

*   While 62% want a third party, **only 15% are "very likely" to vote for one** [2].
*   **57% of Americans are "extremely" or "very" concerned about wasting their vote** on a candidate with no perceived chance of winning [2].
*   **59% are equally concerned about inadvertently helping their least favorite major party candidate win** by peeling votes away from the other major party candidate [2].

This fear is the central mechanism that perpetuates the two-party system. Voters are trapped in a prisoner's dilemma, where their collective desire for a new system is overridden by their individual fear of a worse outcome in the current one.

## 4. Evaluation of Stated Goals

Given the immense structural obstacles, the movement's two primary goals for the 2026 midterms must be evaluated with sober realism.

### Goal 1: Electing Independent Candidates

**Feasibility: Very Low.**

The historical record is unambiguous. Winning a congressional seat as an independent is exceptionally rare. Currently, there are **zero independents in the House of Representatives** and only two in the Senate (Angus King and Bernie Sanders), both of whom come from states with unique political cultures and caucus with the Democrats [10].

The 2024 campaign of Dan Osborn in Nebraska, who earned a remarkable 47% of the vote against an incumbent Republican, is often cited as a model [11]. However, this was a "perfect storm" scenario where the Democratic party effectively stood down and did not run a serious candidate, creating a simple one-on-one race. Replicating these conditions across multiple districts in 2026 is not a scalable strategy.

While a wave of new independent candidates is expected in 2026, inspired by Osborn and the high levels of voter frustration, they will face the full force of the structural and financial barriers detailed above. A victory in a single, highly specific race may be possible, but winning multiple seats to form a meaningful bloc is statistically improbable.

### Goal 2: Persuading Incumbents to Switch to Independent

**Feasibility: Near Zero.**

This objective is strategically flawed and historically unsupported. The data shows a clear and consistent pattern: **incumbents who switch to independent do not survive politically.**

Recent examples include:
*   **Kyrsten Sinema (AZ):** Switched from Democrat to Independent in 2022, did not seek re-election in 2024, facing abysmal approval ratings [10].
*   **Justin Amash (MI):** Switched from Republican to Independent in 2019, lost his committee assignments, and did not seek re-election [8] [10].
*   **Paul Mitchell (MI):** Switched from Republican to Independent in 2020 and retired at the end of his term [10].

Switching parties is an act of political self-immolation. An incumbent who does so is immediately cut off from the party's fundraising apparatus, loses their committee assignments (their primary source of power and influence in Washington), and alienates the base of voters who elected them. They become a pariah to their former party and are viewed with suspicion by the other. There is no incentive for a rational incumbent to take this step.

## 5. Strategic Recommendations: A Sharpian Approach to 2026

A direct assault on the two-party fortress via the 2026 elections is likely to fail, resulting in demoralization and wasted resources. A more strategic approach, grounded in Gene Sharp's principles of nonviolent struggle, would reframe the objective. The goal should not be to *win* the 2026 election, but to *use* the election as a powerful tactic in a larger campaign of political noncooperation.

### Recommendation 1: Shift from Winning to Disrupting

The primary goal should be to **delegitimize the two-party system** in the eyes of the public. This means shifting the focus from electing candidates to demonstrating the system's illegitimacy. The movement should act as a spoiler, a kingmaker, and a chaos agent to prove that the duopoly is no longer tenable.

*   **Targeted Disruption:** Instead of running candidates everywhere, focus resources on a small number of carefully selected House and Senate races. The ideal targets are races where:
    *   The margin between the two major party candidates is expected to be narrow.
    *   There is a high percentage of registered independents.
    *   One or both major party candidates are particularly unpopular.
*   **The Kingmaker Strategy:** The goal in these targeted races is not necessarily to win, but for the independent candidate to garner a larger share of the vote than the margin of victory between the two major party candidates. This provides concrete, undeniable proof that the independent movement decided the outcome. This creates leverage and media attention far more valuable than a single, isolated victory.

### Recommendation 2: Convert Latent Energy into Focused Action

The data shows that millions of Americans are willing to protest against a specific political figure (Donald Trump). The central task is to **convert this anti-Trump energy into a broader, anti-system movement.**

*   **Strategic Messaging:** The movement's messaging must relentlessly connect the problems personified by Trump to the systemic failures of the two-party system that enabled him. The narrative should be: "The problem isn't just the person; it's the system that produces people like this."
*   **Pledge of Non-Cooperation:** The core of the Liberate.US strategy—the national political strike—is sound. The 2026 election cycle should be used as a massive organizing drive to get millions of citizens to sign the "New Declaration of American Independence." This is a direct application of Sharp's method of withdrawing consent.

### Recommendation 3: Begin Building Parallel Institutions

As Sharp noted, a successful nonviolent revolution must not only dismantle the old power structures but also build the new ones that will replace them. The movement cannot wait until after the system collapses to think about what comes next.

*   **The Forward Party Model:** Organizations like the Forward Party and Unite America are already building the infrastructure for an alternative political ecosystem [12]. The American Liberation Movement should partner with, support, and integrate with these efforts. This includes building fundraising networks, candidate training programs, and policy platforms outside the two-party structure.
*   **Focus on Election Reform:** The most durable path to breaking the duopoly is to change the rules of the game. The movement should pour its grassroots energy into state-level campaigns for **Ranked Choice Voting (RCV)** and **nonpartisan open primaries**. These reforms directly attack the structural barriers—like the spoiler effect and closed primaries—that keep the two-party system in power.

## Conclusion

The desire for a political revolution is palpable in the United States. However, desire is not enough. The two-party system is a deeply entrenched power structure that will not be defeated in a single election cycle. A strategy focused on winning seats in 2026 is a recipe for failure.

A more effective, Sharpian strategy recognizes that the 2026 midterms are not the final battle, but a single skirmish in a much longer war. By shifting the objective from winning to disrupting, from electing candidates to delegitimizing the system, the American Liberation Movement can use the election as a powerful tool to demonstrate its power, grow its numbers, and begin the essential work of building a viable alternative. The path to success is not through the front door of Congress in 2026, but through the long, patient, and strategic application of nonviolent political noncooperation.
