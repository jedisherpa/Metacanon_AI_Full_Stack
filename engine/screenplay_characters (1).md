# Screenplay Characters: The Council of Twelve

This document outlines the 12 millennial characters for the screenplay, their backgrounds, traumatic moments, and how these events align them with the 12 epistemologies from the "Hands of the Void" document.

---

### 1. The Rationalist
*   **Name:** Dr. Aris Thorne
*   **Epistemology:** Rationalist
*   **Background:** A brilliant, arrogant data scientist and systems architect for a major financial firm. He designed a complex, automated high-frequency trading algorithm that managed billions of dollars.
*   **Traumatic Moment:** On a day of unusual market volatility (pre-COVID), a subtle flaw in his algorithm, an edge case he dismissed as statistically impossible, triggers a feedback loop. The algorithm goes rogue, executing a cascade of disastrous trades that vaporize half the firm's value in minutes before it can be shut down. Aris watches the code he wrote, a monument to his own logic, tear down his career and reputation in real-time. He is publicly fired and disgraced.
*   **Epistemological Alignment:** The trauma of seeing his own complex, but flawed, logic fail so catastrophically forces Aris into a radical purification of his beliefs. He rejects all heuristics, gut feelings, and emotional data. He dedicates his life to a pure, cold, verifiable logic, building systems of thought that are provably sound from the first principle up. He believes any system that isn't 100% logically verifiable is a bomb waiting to explode.

### 2. The Skeptic
*   **Name:** Maya Jensen
*   **Epistemology:** Skeptic
*   **Background:** A tenacious, idealistic investigative journalist for an online publication. She has a knack for finding sources who won't talk to anyone else.
*   **Traumatic Moment:** Maya uncovers a deeply embedded conspiracy involving a pharmaceutical company suppressing data about a dangerous side effect of a widely used drug. She has documents, whistleblower testimony, everything. The night before her story is to be published, a coordinated smear campaign hits. Deepfaked photos, fabricated emails, and paid-off former colleagues paint her as a fraud. Her publication pulls the story, fires her, and she is legally threatened into silence. The official narrative becomes that she was a disgruntled, unstable fabricator.
*   **Epistemological Alignment:** Betrayed by the very systems of truth and verification she believed in, Maya's worldview shatters. She trusts nothing and no one at face value. Every official statement, every news report, every 
source is now, to her, a potential lie. Her life becomes a relentless cross-examination of reality, constantly asking, "Who benefits from me believing this?" and "What is being hidden?"

### 3. The Ethicist
*   **Name:** Father Ben Carter
*   **Epistemology:** Ethicist
*   **Background:** A young, progressive Catholic priest in a tight-knit urban community. He is beloved for his compassion and his work with the poor and marginalized.
*   **Traumatic Moment:** During the height of the COVID-19 pandemic, Ben is forced to deny last rites to a dying parishioner, a woman he has known his entire life, due to strict hospital and diocesan protocols. He must stand outside her hospital window, helpless, while she dies alone. The institutional rules, designed to protect, become a source of profound moral injury. He feels he has failed in his most sacred duty, choosing institutional obedience over human compassion.
*   **Epistemological Alignment:** The event shatters his faith in rigid, top-down moral systems. He begins to see ethics not as a set of divine or institutional rules to be followed, but as a complex, situational, and deeply personal calculus of compassion and harm reduction. His guiding principle becomes a relentless focus on the immediate human consequence of any action, constantly weighing the spirit of the law against the letter of the law.

### 4. The Economist
*   **Name:** Jia Li
*   **Epistemology:** Economist
*   **Background:** A driven, first-generation immigrant who built a successful small business, a chain of boutique coffee shops, from the ground up. Her entire life has been about maximizing value and minimizing waste.
*   **Traumatic Moment:** The COVID-19 lockdowns are a death sentence for her business. Despite her best efforts—pivoting to delivery, taking out loans, fighting for grants—she is forced to close all her locations, laying off employees she considers family. She loses everything she has built, her life's work and her financial security, due to a systemic shock she had no control over.
*   **Epistemological Alignment:** Jia becomes obsessed with understanding the flow of resources, incentives, and systemic risk. She sees the world as a massive, interconnected economic engine. Her trauma teaches her that individual effort is meaningless without understanding the larger systems at play. She dedicates herself to modeling and predicting economic behavior, not for personal gain, but to understand how to build more resilient and equitable systems.

### 5. The Historian
*   **Name:** Dr. Evelyn Reed
*   **Epistemology:** Historian
*   **Background:** An accomplished professor of post-war European history at a prestigious university. Her life is spent in archives, piecing together narratives from the past.
*   **Traumatic Moment:** While researching for a new book, she uncovers a hidden set of documents in a forgotten archive that proves a celebrated historical figure, a national hero, was secretly a war criminal who betrayed his own people for personal gain. When she presents her findings, the academic community and the public turn on her. She is accused of revisionism and trying to tear down a national icon. Her university pressures her to recant, and her reputation is destroyed.
*   **Epistemological Alignment:** The backlash teaches her that history is not a static collection of facts, but a constantly contested narrative shaped by power in the present. She becomes fiercely committed to the idea that the past is never dead, and that understanding its patterns is the only way to understand the present. She sees every event, every political decision, every social trend as an echo of something that has happened before.

### 6. The Scientist
*   **Name:** Kenji Tanaka
*   **Epistemology:** Scientist
*   **Background:** A brilliant but methodical biochemist working in a corporate R&D lab. He believes in the slow, steady, peer-reviewed process of scientific discovery.
*   **Traumatic Moment:** Kenji is part of a team that discovers a revolutionary new water purification method. The corporation, seeing more profit in their existing, less effective technologies, buries the research, classifies it as a trade secret, and reassigns the team. Kenji is forced to watch as a discovery that could save millions of lives is locked away in a vault for the sake of quarterly earnings. He is bound by a crushing NDA.
*   **Epistemological Alignment:** This betrayal of the scientific ideal—the pursuit of knowledge for the betterment of humanity—radicalizes him. He quits his job and dedicates himself to the principles of open-source science and verifiable, repeatable experimentation. He believes that any claim, no matter how credentialed the source, is worthless without a transparent, testable methodology. Truth is a process, not a proclamation.

### 7. The Philosopher
*   **Name:** Chloe Dubois
*   **Epistemology:** Philosopher
*   **Background:** A PhD dropout who works as a bartender in a dive bar. She is whip-smart, well-read, and deeply cynical about the value of abstract thought in the "real world."
*   **Traumatic Moment:** Her younger brother, a bright and sensitive young man, falls into a deep depression and, after a series of existential crises, takes his own life. Chloe finds his journals, filled with frantic, unanswered questions about meaning, purpose, and the nature of existence—the very questions she used to debate in her seminars.
*   **Epistemological Alignment:** Her brother's death makes philosophy terrifyingly real. It is no longer an academic game. She becomes obsessed with the foundational questions of being, meaning, and value. She sees the world as a complex web of underlying assumptions and first principles. She believes that most human suffering comes from a failure to define our terms and understand the fundamental "why" behind our actions.

### 8. The Futurist
*   **Name:** Jax
*   **Epistemology:** Futurist
*   **Background:** A self-taught coder, bio-hacker, and trend-spotter living on the fringes. He's always been obsessed with what's next, living one step ahead of the curve.
*   **Traumatic Moment:** Jax is an early adopter of a neural interface implant from a small, cutting-edge startup. The device allows him to access information and communicate with unprecedented speed. One day, the startup is acquired by a massive tech conglomerate, and as part of the merger, they push a mandatory firmware "update" that bricks his implant, leaving him with a useless piece of technology in his skull and a sudden, jarring disconnection from the digital world he had integrated with.
*   **Epistemological Alignment:** The experience teaches him that the future isn't just about technological progress; it's about the control and ownership of that progress. He becomes a fierce advocate for decentralized technology and predictive modeling. He dedicates himself to tracking vectors of change—technological, social, political—to forecast potential futures and identify inflection points where a small change can have a massive impact.

### 9. The Legalist
*   **Name:** Marcus Cole
*   **Epistemology:** Legal
*   **Background:** A sharp, ambitious corporate lawyer who prides himself on his ability to navigate and exploit complex legal frameworks for his clients.
*   **Traumatic Moment:** Marcus's family is financially ruined by a predatory, but technically legal, subprime mortgage scheme. He tries to fight it in court, but discovers that the contracts are ironclad. The system he has dedicated his life to, the system he is an expert in, is used to crush the people he loves, all while following its own rules perfectly.
*   **Epistemological Alignment:** His faith in the law as a just system is shattered. He now sees it as a system of code, a set of rules that can be manipulated and exploited. He becomes obsessed with the structure of rules themselves—how they are written, who they benefit, and where their loopholes lie. He believes that justice is not about morality, but about the precise, rigorous application and analysis of the rules as written.

### 10. The Psychologist
*   **Name:** Dr. Lena Petrova
*   **Epistemology:** Psychologist
*   **Background:** A compassionate and insightful therapist specializing in trauma.
*   **Traumatic Moment:** Lena works with a veteran suffering from severe PTSD. She uses all the established, evidence-based therapies, but her patient makes no progress. One day, the veteran has a complete breakdown in her office, revealing a core trauma he had never been able to articulate, a memory so deeply buried that it was inaccessible to conventional therapy. In that moment, Lena realizes the limitations of her own models of the human mind.
*   **Epistemological Alignment:** The event forces her to look beyond established psychological frameworks. She becomes fascinated by the hidden drivers of human behavior—the unconscious biases, the cognitive blind spots, the emotional heuristics that shape our decisions without our awareness. She dedicates herself to understanding the "why" behind the "what" of human action, believing that all behavior, no matter how irrational it seems, has an underlying psychological logic.

### 11. The Sociologist
*   **Name:** David Chen
*   **Epistemology:** Sociologist
*   **Background:** An urban planner and community organizer working to revitalize a low-income neighborhood.
*   **Traumatic Moment:** David's project is a huge success. He helps secure funding for a new community center, a park, and affordable housing. But within a few years, the neighborhood becomes "cool." Gentrification kicks in, property values skyrocket, and the very residents he fought to help are priced out and displaced by wealthier newcomers. His work, intended to strengthen a community, ends up destroying it.
*   **Epistemological Alignment:** David is devastated to see how his well-intentioned actions had unintended, destructive consequences at a group level. He becomes obsessed with understanding the dynamics of social structures, cultural norms, and group identity. He sees the world not as a collection of individuals, but as a series of overlapping and competing groups, each with its own rules and power dynamics. He believes that individual intent is irrelevant in the face of powerful social forces.

### 12. The Political Animal
*   **Name:** Isabella Rossi
*   **Epistemology:** Political
*   **Background:** A savvy, ambitious political strategist and campaign manager.
*   **Traumatic Moment:** Isabella orchestrates a brilliant campaign for a presidential candidate she truly believes in. They are on the verge of winning when a rival campaign leaks a piece of damaging, but out-of-context, opposition research. The media runs with it, creating a firestorm that sinks the campaign in the final days. Isabella watches as a contest of ideas and policy is decided by a single, well-timed, and manipulative power play.
*   **Epistemological Alignment:** The loss hardens her. She realizes that politics is not about being right; it's about winning. She sees the world as a zero-sum game of power, influence, and leverage. She becomes a master of reading power dynamics, understanding who has leverage, who wants it, and what they are willing to do to get it. Every interaction, for her, is a negotiation of power.
